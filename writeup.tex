\section{Constructing Scaling Laws (\texttt{scaling\_laws})}
% ============================================================

\subsection{What I did and why}

I had a 12-hour B200 budget. I split the work into three phases so that I could tune the learning rate cheaply, then transfer it to wider models, and only then spend the bulk of the budget on points for a scaling law. The overall logic follows Kaplan et al.~\cite{kaplan2020}, Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022}, and Yang et al.~\cite{yang2022}, but each step below states what I chose and why.

Before the main pipeline I ran one short job. I did this so that I would not waste hours on a broken API contract, a broken result path, or a wrong guess about how fast the cluster really trains (MFU). That run gave me a sanity check on all three.

For Phase~1 I needed a single peak learning rate to reuse everywhere. Tuning it on a tiny model is cheap. I used the smallest architecture the API allows: $\texttt{hidden\_size} = \texttt{num\_attention\_heads} \cdot \texttt{head\_dim}$ with $\texttt{head\_dim} = 64$, which gives $d_\text{model} = 128$, $L = 2$, and $1.77\,\text{M}$ non-embedding parameters. I swept nine learning rates on a log-spaced grid from $3 \times 10^{-4}$ to $3 \times 10^{-1}$. Each run trained on about $16\,\text{M}$ tokens with a cosine schedule matched to that length, as in Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} (Approach~3). I set the top of the sweep high enough that the largest rate would diverge. If the best value sat on the upper edge, I would not know whether a higher rate could be better; an interior optimum is easier to trust.

Phase~2 had to tell me how to scale that rate when $d_\text{model}$ grows. Yang et al.~\cite{yang2022} justify $\mu$P, where the optimal rate is width-invariant, but the full setup is not available through the assignment API. I therefore used the simpler width scaling they and Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} discuss,
\begin{equation}
    \eta(d) = \eta_\text{proxy} \cdot \left( \frac{d_\text{proxy}}{d} \right)^{\!\gamma},
\end{equation}
and I estimated $\gamma$ from data instead of fixing it to the common guess $\gamma = 1$. I ran small grids of three rates at $d_\text{model} \in \{320, 512\}$, centered on the prediction from $\gamma = 1$. Both first grids put the winner at the low end ($\times 0.5/\times 1/\times 2$ relative to a center), so I ran a second pass at $\times 0.25$ and $\times 0.5$ around the previous best. I stopped once both widths had an interior best, because then the estimate of $\gamma$ is not pinned to a grid edge.

For Phase~3 I froze $\eta_\text{proxy}$ from Phase~1 and $\gamma$ from Phase~2. I trained six model families from $1.77\,\text{M}$ to $116\,\text{M}$ non-embedding parameters at compute targets $C \in \{10^{18}, 3\times 10^{18}, 10^{19}\}$ FLOPs; the exact pairing of family and $C$ is in Table~\ref{tab:phase3-design}. I kept $d_\text{model}/L \approx 50$ because Kaplan et al.~\cite{kaplan2020} find that aspect ratio moves results only weakly at fixed parameter count, so I did not want to spend budget on exotic shapes. I also made sure that at $10^{18}$ and $3\times 10^{18}$ FLOPs at least four families appear. Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} (Approach~2) use IsoFLOPs cuts to check a parametric fit; with several families at the same $C$ I can make the same kind of comparison.

The hosted API reserves \texttt{max\_runtime\_seconds} up front and refunds what I do not use. I set each job's cap from $\texttt{safety\_factor} \cdot C / (\texttt{MFU} \cdot \texttt{peak\_FLOPs})$ with $\texttt{MFU} = 0.4$ and $\texttt{safety\_factor} = 1.5$, clipped at two hours, so that planned training had headroom against slowdowns. I still submitted Phase~3 in three waves (five, five, three jobs) so refunds from finished runs returned budget before I queued the next wave. That order was a practical way to stay inside the 12-hour pool without hand-tuning every reservation.

\subsection{Phase 1 and Phase 2 outcomes}

Phase~1 peaked at $\eta_\text{proxy} = 2.25 \times 10^{-2}$ with validation loss $5.85$. The endpoints $3 \times 10^{-4}$ and $3 \times 10^{-1}$ landed at $7.98$ and $6.68$, so the best point is clearly inside the sweep.

Phase~2 gave best rates $4.50 \times 10^{-3}$ at $d_\text{model} = 320$ and $2.81 \times 10^{-3}$ at $d_\text{model} = 512$. Averaging the implied $\gamma$ from both widths,
\begin{equation}
    \gamma \;=\; \frac{1}{2} \sum_{d \in \{320,\, 512\}} \frac{\log\bigl(\eta(d)/\eta_\text{proxy}\bigr)}{\log(d_\text{proxy}/d)} \;=\; 1.628.
\end{equation}
That is steeper than $\gamma = 1$, but Yang et al.~\cite{yang2022} (Figure~1) already show that this simple rule can move the best rate by nearly an order of magnitude when width grows, so I did not treat $\gamma = 1$ as a hard truth. I carried $\gamma = 1.628$ into Phase~3.

\subsection{Phase 3, salvage, and the anchor check}

Every Phase~3 run hit my wall-time cap. From eval throughput I inferred MFU between $1.6\%$ and $4.7\%$, far below the $40\%$ I assumed when sizing \texttt{max\_runtime}. Three of the smallest jobs never logged an eval block; ten others returned one to three partial validation losses.

Dropping those runs would leave almost no data, so I kept them and estimated trained tokens as $D_\text{actual} = (\texttt{evals\_completed} / 16) \cdot D_\text{planned}$ for each job. I fit the law on $(N, D_\text{actual}, L_\text{partial})$. The worry is upward bias: if training stops while the cosine schedule is still near the peak rate, loss can look worse than for a run that decays on schedule.

I tested that worry on $(d_\text{model}, L) = (576, 10)$. I re-ran training with \texttt{total\_train\_tokens} set to the same $D_\text{actual}$ as the truncated job so the cosine could finish (final rate $0.1\,\eta_\text{peak}$). The truncated partial log gave $L = 3.7820$; the matched schedule gave $L = 3.7813$. The gap is $0.001$ nats, so at this $(N, D)$ the salvage choice does not materially shift the loss. I therefore kept $D_\text{actual}$ for the fit.

\subsection{Fitting the scaling law}

I followed Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} Approach~3 (Equations~2--3),
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{L}(N, D) \;=\; E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}}.
\end{equation}
I fit $(E, A, B, \alpha, \beta)$ by minimizing Huber loss on log-residuals with $\delta = 10^{-3}$, matching Hoffmann et al., using L-BFGS-B. Non-convex fits can land in local minima, so I repeated the fit from eight starting points covering $\log E \in \{\log 1, \log 2\}$, $\alpha \in \{0.3, 0.5\}$, and $\beta \in \{0.3, 0.5\}$, and I kept the solution with the lowest objective.

On the ten salvaged Phase~3 points,
\begin{equation*}
    E = 3.376, \quad A = 9.66 \times 10^{6}, \quad B = 9.64 \times 10^{6}, \quad \alpha = 1.049, \quad \beta = 0.857.
\end{equation*}
My $\alpha$ and $\beta$ exceed Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022}'s Chinchilla values ($\alpha = 0.34$, $\beta = 0.28$), but the ratio $\alpha/\beta = 1.22$ matches theirs ($1.21$) almost exactly. Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} Equation~5 shows that the compute-optimal split between $N$ and $D$ depends only on $\alpha/\beta$, so my prediction for how to divide compute between model and data stays aligned with Chinchilla even though the raw exponents differ. The narrower $N$ span in my data ($5.4\times$ versus $230\times$ in Hoffmann et al.) is the natural reason the individual exponents look sharper.

Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-law-fit} shows that residuals stay small across the ten points ($\max |r| = 0.048$ nats, RMSE $0.025$ nats), so the parametric form describes my measured range well. I would still be cautious far outside it. My parameter counts only run from $21.7\,\text{M}$ to $116\,\text{M}$ ($5.4\times$), and Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} warn that exponent estimates move a lot when the fit range is narrow. The observed $D/N$ ratios fall in $[12, 220]$ with most runs above the Chinchilla-optimal $D/N \approx 20$, so the surface may be less reliable if I extrapolate to very different data-to-parameter ratios.

\subsection{Prediction for 48 B200-hours and why it changed}

The unconstrained Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} recipe at $7.78 \times 10^{20}$ FLOPs (48 B200-hours at peak) predicts $N^{*} = 1.23 \times 10^{9}$ and $D^{*} = 1.05 \times 10^{11}$. I could not use that plan: at the MFU I actually saw ($\approx 5\%$), finishing that training would need on the order of $211$ wall-clock hours, which breaks a 48-hour cap.

I therefore minimized $\widehat{L}(N, D)$ subject to $6 N D = M \cdot \texttt{peak\_FLOPs} \cdot 48\,\text{h}$. I set $M = 0.05$ from my largest completed runs ($116\,\text{M}$ parameters) because that is the only hard evidence I had; larger models often improve MFU on tensor cores, but guessing higher without measurements felt optimistic. I then shaved $D$ by $20\%$ below the constrained optimum to leave slack ($\approx 9.6$ hours at $M = 0.05$) so the cosine schedule can finish even if throughput is a bit worse than expected.

Table~\ref{tab:hyperparameters} lists the configuration. The implied validation loss from the fit is
\begin{equation}
    L^{*} \;=\; \widehat{L}(3.13 \times 10^{8},\, 1.66 \times 10^{10}) \;=\; 3.4051.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Hyperparameters for the leaderboard run}

I set depth and width in the spirit of Kaplan et al.~\cite{kaplan2020}. I matched Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2022} on training mechanics: cosine to the full token count, $10\times$ LR decay, AdamW with $\beta_2 = 0.95$. Peak learning rate comes from Yang et al.~\cite{yang2022} through the width scaling with my measured $\gamma = 1.628$, giving $\eta = 6.90 \times 10^{-4}$ from $\eta_\text{proxy} = 2.25 \times 10^{-2}$ at $d_\text{proxy} = 128$.
